# Repository audit — July 2026

A module-by-module and integration-level audit of token-warden's measurement
logic, cross-checked against the closed-loop LLM-optimization literature
(primarily "LLM-driven compiler pass ordering", arXiv 2511.00592v2 — an
LLM proposes, a deterministic system measures, results feed back). Three
zero-token improvements were implemented from it (v0.32.0); the rest are
recorded here as validated design or as future falsification experiments.

## The architecture is the right one

The paper's central result is that the architecture token-warden already has —
**LLM proposes, deterministic verifier measures, only measured survivors
persist** — beats letting the LLM act directly on the artifact (their direct
code-generation arm had a 17.9% silent-correctness-failure rate at 5.3× the
token cost). token-warden's equivalent guarantees:

- a candidate rule is never injected until benchmarked (invariant #1),
- the golden suite is frozen and versioned (invariants #4, #5),
- eviction is mandatory and evicted rules are the negative dataset.

Nothing in the audit contradicted this. The findings below are all about
sharpening the loop, not changing its shape.

## Module-by-module findings

### src/select.ts — the statistical core

**Sound:** the within-task standard error (v0.23.0) is the correct estimand
for a frozen suite and shrinks as 1/sqrt(runs); Neyman top-up allocation
(v0.24.0) spends the run budget where the variance is; the calibration-vetoed
robust SE stays a report-only tail-risk flag (v0.30.0); the z=2 confidence
gate holds the false-positive rate at ~2-3%.

**Found and fixed — re-audit churn (one-strike retention).** Admission
demands `delta >= bar + z*SE`, but retention tested only the point estimate
against the bar. Because the bar (2× rent, tens of tokens) is tiny next to
the SE (thousands of tokens), every re-audit of a *genuine* earner was a coin
flip with a non-trivial tail: by regression to the mean, a rule truly earning
6,000 tokens/run failed ~14% of its re-audits (gaussian noise model) and was
expected to churn out within ~7 cycles. The live database actually did this:
the Grep rule, admitted at +3,673, was evicted on a single −9,215 re-audit
draw. Keep-when-uncertain is not a fix — rent << SE means a *dead* rule is
always "uncertain" relative to the bar and would never leave.

**Fix: two-strike probation.** The first non-regression sub-threshold
re-audit puts the rule on probation (kept, flagged, strike recorded in the
DB); a second *consecutive* sub-threshold re-audit evicts; a passing re-audit
clears the strike. A regression still evicts immediately (safety invariant).
Calibration harness verdict (validation/calibration.ts, churn section):

| true effect | P(sub-threshold)/cycle | 1-strike lifetime | 2-strike lifetime |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (dead rule) | 50.7% | 2.0 cycles | 5.9 cycles |
| 3,000 tok/run | 29.9% | 3.3 cycles | 14.5 cycles |
| 6,000 tok/run | 13.7% | 7.3 cycles | 61.0 cycles |
| 12,000 tok/run | 1.6% | 63.5 cycles | 4,094.7 cycles |

(gaussian model, runs=3; the derailment model shows the same shape.) The
asymmetry is the point: a dead rule pays ~25 tokens/session of rent for ~4
extra cycles — trivial — while a real earner's expected lifetime grows by
roughly the inverse of its per-cycle failure rate.

**Found and fixed — survivorship bias in the savings mean.** Savings means
use completed runs only (invariant #3), so a rule whose failed runs are
excluded looks cheaper than it is. A full per-task failure was already the
regression eviction, but a *partial* completion drop (2/2 completed without
the rule, 1/2 with it) silently flattered the mean. Fixed as a report-only
`COMPLETION-DROP` flag comparing completion *rates* per task (rates, not
counts, because a Neyman top-up legitimately gives the measured side more
runs). Report-only on purpose: making it a gate input would let one flaky
run veto a rule, and the calibration harness has no completion model to
validate a threshold against yet.

### src/distill.ts — the proposer

**Sound:** p75 trigger, strict JSON parsing, trigram dedupe against all past
rules (evicted included), the same-result constraint in the prompt.

**Found and fixed — the feedback loop was half-open.** The paper's RQ6
result (23–40% of total improvement comes from feeding the *measured
outcomes* of past proposals back to the proposer) exposed the gap: the
prompt fed active rules back ("don't repeat, find new") but said nothing
about the *rejected* proposals — the distiller could re-derive the same
falsified idea in different words forever (trigram dedupe only catches
near-verbatim repeats). Fixed: `buildPrompt` now receives the agent's most
recent evicted rules (≤ 8) with their measured deltas and eviction reasons,
plus instruction on how to read them — sub-threshold means the targeted
behavior was too small a waste source, regression means the rule traded
correctness for tokens. This is the verdict-grounded, closed-loop signal.

**Deferred — best-of-K sampling (paper RQ1).** Sampling the distiller K
times at temperature and benchmarking the best proposal exploits proposal
stochasticity (their geomean improved 2.66× → 3.54× at K=30). For
token-warden the *distill* call is cheap but each extra distinct candidate
costs a full bench (~50–100k tokens), so K must stay small (2–3) and dedupe
must collapse near-identical samples first. Worth a measured experiment, not
a default. Recorded in the roadmap.

### src/bench.ts, src/transcript.ts, src/db.ts, src/cohort.ts, src/cost.ts

Audited; no correctness defects found. Specifically checked: transcript
dedup by message id (JSONL re-emission cannot double-count), frozen run1
baselines, receipt accounting using the newest receipt per rule (so dollar
accounting self-corrects after re-audits), cohort validation staying
observational (never admits a rule). The bench's spawn boundary remains the
intentional integration seam (~45% unit coverage, covered by e2e smoke).

## Statistics hygiene notes (from the paper, held against our stack)

- **Median-of-pool / geometric mean:** appropriate for the paper's ratio
  metrics across heterogeneous programs; token-warden's estimand is an
  *additive* per-run token delta on a fixed suite, so the arithmetic mean +
  within-task SE is the right choice. No change.
- **Bootstrap CIs:** unnecessary while the within-task normal approximation
  holds (runs are cheap to add via top-up); revisit if run distributions turn
  multi-modal.
- **Winner's curse / multiple comparisons:** with ≤ 3 candidates per
  invocation and z=2 per decision, the family-wise false-positive rate is
  bounded at ~7-8% per invocation worst-case; acceptable, but if
  MAX_CANDIDATES_PER_INVOCATION ever grows, a per-invocation Bonferroni
  z-adjustment is the first knob.

## Falsification experiments still worth running (token-spending)

1. **Best-of-K distillation** (paper RQ1): K=3 samples, dedupe, bench the
   survivors; measure whether the extra bench cost buys a higher survival
   rate than K=1. Success metric: surviving-rule tokens/run per bench token
   spent, K=3 vs K=1.
2. **Rule-body compression A/B:** rewrite a surviving rule to half its
   character count (rent is length/4) and re-bench; if the delta holds, rent
   drops and marginal rules clear the bar. Cheap, bounded.
3. **Out-of-fixture confirmation:** the cohort module already measures real
   work; the missing piece is running it *after* the next production dogfood
   window and comparing its verdict to the fixture verdict for the same rule
   set (does fixture survival predict production savings?).

## What shipped in v0.32.0

- Two-strike probation for re-audits (src/select.ts, migration #13
  `rules.probation`), validated by the calibration churn tables above.
- Verdict-grounded distiller feedback: recent evicted rules with measured
  deltas and reasons in the distill prompt (src/distill.ts, src/db.ts
  `recentEvictedRules`).
- Completion-drop flag (report-only) in `assessDelta` and the selector
  output.
- Calibration harness churn section (validation/calibration.ts) — the
  zero-token proof for the retention-policy change.
